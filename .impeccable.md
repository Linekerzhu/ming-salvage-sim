## Design Context

### Users
Players who want a long-form, systems-driven Ming dynasty political strategy simulation. They are reading reports, interrogating ministers, drafting decrees, and trying to make scarce resources land inside a failing state machine rather than choosing from simple preset options.

### Brand Personality
Grave, historical, bureaucratic, and tense. The interface should feel like imperial records, court pressure, and crisis management, with enough clarity that the player can reason through consequences instead of drowning in ornament.

### Aesthetic Direction
Late Ming court strategy HUD: aged paper, cinnabar seals, lacquered wood, ink, gold trim, and painterly historical assets. Avoid generic modern SaaS cards, neon dark gradients, and ornamental effects that obscure the game state.

### Design Principles
- Make consequences legible: every decree should expose cost, risk, affected systems, and likely execution friction.
- Preserve historical texture without sacrificing scanability; court flavor supports decisions, it does not bury them.
- Keep numbers few but meaningful: headline indicators show survival pressure, details open only when useful.
- Reward deliberate play: surface tradeoffs, pending drafts, unresolved crises, and recent deltas at the moment the emperor must act.
- Treat feedback as gameplay: month-end changes, minister reactions, warnings, and blockers should make the state machine feel alive.
